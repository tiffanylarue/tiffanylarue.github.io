My main takeaways from the readings this week were:

On the topic of God (from last week), it was interesting how philosophers were defining God as the thing that's greater than all other things. It made me thing of how humans categorize things in general. i.e. All of these parts together is a computer. Then there is a subset of parts that together are the CPU, a different set that together makes up RAM, etc. It could even be thought of like a Venn diagram. i.e. There are neural networks. Then beyond that, there is ML. And beyond that is AI, encompassing all of it. Similarly, God is the biggest circle, surrounding everything we know, name, see, etc.
Many of our courses (Discrete Math, Data Science, AI) covered Bayes' Theory and its importance in probability with new evidence. Interestingly, the theory was developed back in the 18th century and was used to debate whether miracles were believable! In 1748, philosopher David Hume wrote about how we can never have sufficient prior evidence to justify believing miracles happened. Thirteen years later, Thomas Bayes developed his theory. Then two years later, moral philosopher Richard Price used Bayes' Theory to respond to Hume's skepticism, and say that a strong enough testimony of a miracle would outweigh a low prior probability.  
Philosophy was also used to understand what makes someone guilty, and what laws there should be. What makes someone negligent and reckless? What if someone commits a crime but they didn't know better or had no better option? A lot of these questions come into play in ethics around computer science - e.g. social media companies, AI companies, etc.
